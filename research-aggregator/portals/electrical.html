    Electrical Fundamentals

    Electricity is the flow of electrons through a conductor. Understanding basic electrical theory is essential for safe and effective electrical work.

    Ohm's Law

    Ohm's Law is the fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance:

    Ohm's Law Formulas
    V = I √ó R    (Voltage = Current √ó Resistance)
I = V / R    (Current = Voltage / Resistance)
R = V / I    (Resistance = Voltage / Current)

Where:
  V = Voltage (Volts, V)
  I = Current (Amperes, A)
  R = Resistance (Ohms, Œ©)

Example:
  A circuit has 120V and 10Œ© resistance
  I = 120V / 10Œ© = 12 Amps

    Electrical Units

    • Voltage (V) - Electrical pressure or potential difference
    • Amperage (A) - Rate of electron flow (current)
    • Resistance (Œ©) - Opposition to current flow
    • Wattage (W) - Power (W = V √ó A)

    Types of Current

    • DC (Direct Current) - Current flows in one direction (batteries, solar panels)
    • AC (Alternating Current) - Current reverses direction cyclically (household power, 60Hz in US)

    Safety First

    Electricity can kill. Always de-energize circuits before working on them. Use proper PPE. Test circuits with a voltmeter before touching. When in doubt, call a licensed electrician.

    Electrical Circuits

    An electrical circuit is a complete path for current to flow from the source, through the load, and back to the source.

    Circuit Types

    Series Circuits

    Components connected in a single path. Current is the same through all components.

    • Total resistance = R1 + R2 + R3...
    • If one component fails, circuit breaks
    • Voltage divides among components

    Parallel Circuits

    Components connected across each other. Voltage is the same across all branches.

    • Total resistance: 1/Rt = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3...
    • If one branch fails, others continue
    • Current divides among branches

    Circuit Components

    • Circuit Breaker - Overcurrent protection device, can be reset
    • Fuse - Overcurrent protection, must be replaced
    • GFCI - Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter, prevents shock
    • AFCI - Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter, prevents fires
    • Switch - Controls circuit on/off
    • Receptacle (Outlet) - Connection point for plug-in devices

    Wire Sizing

    AWG (American Wire Gauge) - Lower number = larger wire. Size based on circuit amperage:

    National Electrical Code (NEC)

    The NEC (National Electrical Code) is the standard for safe electrical installation in the United States. Updated every three years (2020, 2023, 2026...).

    Key NEC Articles

    • Article 100 - Definitions
    • Article 110 - Requirements for electrical installations
    • Article 210 - Branch circuits
    • Article 220 - Load calculations
    • Article 240 - Overcurrent protection
    • Article 250 - Grounding and bonding
    • Article 300 - Wiring methods
    • Article 310 - Conductors
    • Article 334 - NM cable (Romex)
    • Article 404 - Switches
    • Article 406 - Receptacles
    • Article 408 - Panels
    • Article 680 - Swimming pools

    GFCI Requirements (NEC 210.8)

    GFCI protection required in:

    • Bathrooms
    • Kitchens (within 6 feet of sink)
    • Garages and accessory buildings
    • Outdoors
    • Crawl spaces and unfinished basements
    • Laundry areas
    • Within 6 feet of sinks (NEC 2020+)

    AFCI Requirements (NEC 210.12)

    AFCI protection required in:

    • Bedrooms
    • Living rooms, parlors, dens
    • Dining rooms
    • Hallways, closets
    • Most dwelling unit locations (NEC 2020+)

    Troubleshooting

    Systematic troubleshooting finds electrical problems efficiently and safely.

    Essential Test Equipment

    • Multimeter - Measures voltage, current, resistance
    • Non-contact voltage tester - Detects live circuits safely
    • Clamp meter - Measures current without breaking circuit
    • Circuit tracer - Identifies which breaker controls a circuit
    • Receptacle tester - Quick outlet check (open ground, reversed polarity)

    Common Problems & Solutions

    Dead Outlet

    1. Check breaker - reset if tripped
    2. Check GFCI - may be on same circuit
    3. Test outlet with voltmeter
    4. Check connections at outlet
    5. Check upstream outlets

    Tripping Breaker

    • Overload - Too many devices on circuit
    • Short circuit - Hot touching neutral or ground
    • Ground fault - Current leaking to ground
    • Arc fault - Damaged wire arcing

    Flickering Lights

    • Loose bulb or fixture connection
    • Loose wire connections
    • Bad breaker or main lug connection
    • Utility issue (multiple fixtures)
    ‚úÖ

    Troubleshooting Approach

    Start at the load, work back to the source. Verify presence or absence of voltage at each point. Use process of elimination to isolate the problem.

    Commercial & Industrial Systems

    Commercial and industrial electrical systems are more complex than residential.

    Three-Phase Power

    Three-phase power delivers more power efficiently for large loads:

    • 208V 3-phase - Common in commercial buildings
    • 480V 3-phase - Industrial motors, large HVAC
    • Wye (Y) configuration - Neutral available
    • Delta configuration - No neutral, higher voltage

    Motor Controls

    • Starters - Control motor starting/stopping
    • Contactors - Heavy-duty switching
    • VFDs - Variable Frequency Drives for speed control
    • Overloads - Thermal motor protection

    Lighting Systems

    • LED - Energy efficient, long life
    • Emergency lighting - Battery backup required
    • Exit signs - Code required locations
    • Lighting controls - Occupancy sensors, photocells, timers

    Safety & Best Practices

    Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)

    LOTO procedures prevent accidental energization:

    1. Notify affected personnel
    2. Identify energy sources
    3. Isolate and de-energize
    4. Apply lock and tag
    5. Verify zero energy state
    6. Perform work
    7. Remove locks when complete

    Personal Protective Equipment

    • Safety glasses
    • Insulated gloves (rated for voltage)
    • Arc-flash rated clothing
    • Hard hat (where required)
    • Insulated tools

    Arc Flash Safety

    Arc flash can cause severe burns. Higher voltage = greater risk. Always:

    • Check arc flash labels on equipment
    • Wear appropriate PPE for the hazard level
    • Maintain safe working distance
    • De-energize when possible

    Licensing & Career Development

    Electrician License Levels

    • Apprentice - Learning under supervision (typically 4 years)
    • Journeyman - Completed apprenticeship, licensed to work independently
    • Master Electrician - Advanced license, can supervise and pull permits
    • Electrical Contractor - Business license to bid and perform work

    Apprenticeship Programs

    • IBEW/NECA - Union apprenticeship programs
    • ABC - Associated Builders and Contractors programs
    • IEC - Independent Electrical Contractors programs
    • Community colleges - Technical training programs

    Continuing Education

    • NEC code updates (every 3 years)
    • Specialty certifications (low voltage, fire alarm)
    • State-required CE hours for license renewal
