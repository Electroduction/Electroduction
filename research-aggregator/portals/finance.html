    Introduction to Finance

    Finance is the study of money management, including activities such as investing, borrowing, lending, budgeting, saving, and forecasting. It encompasses personal finance, corporate finance, and public finance.

    Core Concepts

    The foundation of finance rests on several key principles:

    • Time Value of Money - A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow
    • Risk-Return Tradeoff - Higher potential returns require accepting higher risk
    • Diversification - Don't put all your eggs in one basket
    • Compound Interest - Interest earning interest over time
    üí°

    The Power of Compounding

    $10,000 invested at 7% annual return becomes $76,123 in 30 years. Time is your greatest asset in investing. Start early.

    Types of Finance

    • Personal Finance - Individual money management, budgeting, retirement planning
    • Corporate Finance - Business funding, capital structure, financial decisions
    • Public Finance - Government revenue, expenditure, and debt management
    • Behavioral Finance - Psychology's impact on financial decisions

    Financial Statements Analysis

    Understanding financial statements is essential for evaluating business health and making investment decisions.

    The Three Core Statements

    Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

    Shows profitability over a period. Key items:

    • Revenue - Total sales/income
    • Gross Profit - Revenue minus cost of goods sold
    • Operating Income (EBIT) - Gross profit minus operating expenses
    • Net Income - Bottom line after all expenses and taxes

    Balance Sheet

    Snapshot of financial position at a point in time. The fundamental equation:

    Accounting Equation
    Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

Assets: What the company owns
  - Current Assets (cash, inventory, receivables)
  - Non-current Assets (property, equipment, intangibles)

Liabilities: What the company owes
  - Current Liabilities (payables, short-term debt)
  - Non-current Liabilities (long-term debt, leases)

Equity: Owners' stake
  - Common Stock
  - Retained Earnings

    Cash Flow Statement

    Shows cash movements in three categories:

    • Operating Activities - Cash from core business operations
    • Investing Activities - Capital expenditures, acquisitions
    • Financing Activities - Debt, equity, dividends

    Investment Fundamentals

    Investing is putting money to work to generate returns over time. Understanding different asset classes and their characteristics is fundamental.

    Asset Classes

    Equities (Stocks)

    Equities represent ownership in companies. Higher risk, higher potential return. Types include:

    • Growth Stocks - Companies expected to grow faster than market
    • Value Stocks - Undervalued companies trading below intrinsic value
    • Dividend Stocks - Companies paying regular dividends
    • Small/Mid/Large Cap - Companies by market capitalization

    Fixed Income (Bonds)

    Bonds are debt instruments where you lend money in exchange for interest payments and principal return. Generally lower risk than stocks.

    • Government Bonds - Treasuries (safest), municipal bonds
    • Corporate Bonds - Investment grade vs. high yield (junk)
    • Bond characteristics - Yield, duration, credit rating

    Alternative Investments

    • Real Estate - Direct ownership or REITs
    • Commodities - Gold, oil, agricultural products
    • Private Equity - Non-public company investments
    • Hedge Funds - Alternative strategies (long-short, arbitrage)

    Investment Strategies

    Passive Investing

    Buy and hold diversified index funds. Low cost, tax-efficient. Based on efficient market hypothesis.

    Active Investing

    Attempt to outperform market through stock picking, market timing, or factor investing.

    Dollar-Cost Averaging

    Invest fixed amounts regularly regardless of price. Reduces impact of volatility.

    ‚úÖ

    Evidence-Based Investing

    Research shows most active managers underperform indexes over time. Low-cost index funds are recommended for most investors. Focus on costs, diversification, and staying invested.

    Valuation Methods

    Valuation is determining the current worth of an asset or company. Essential for investment decisions.

    Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

    DCF calculates present value of expected future cash flows:

    DCF Formula
    Value = Œ£ [CFt / (1 + r)^t] + Terminal Value / (1 + r)^n

Where:
  CFt = Cash flow in year t
  r = Discount rate (WACC)
  n = Projection period

Terminal Value = CFn √ó (1 + g) / (r - g)
  g = Long-term growth rate (usually 2-3%)

Steps:
1. Project free cash flows for 5-10 years
2. Calculate terminal value
3. Discount all to present value
4. Sum = Intrinsic value

    Relative Valuation (Multiples)

    Compare to similar companies using ratios:

    • P/E (Price/Earnings) - Most common, compare to peers/history
    • EV/EBITDA - Enterprise value to operating earnings
    • P/S (Price/Sales) - Useful for unprofitable companies
    • P/B (Price/Book) - Asset-heavy industries

    WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)

    WACC is the discount rate used in DCF, representing the cost of all capital sources:

    WACC Formula
    WACC = (E/V √ó Re) + (D/V √ó Rd √ó (1-T))

Where:
  E = Market value of equity
  D = Market value of debt
  V = E + D (total capital)
  Re = Cost of equity (CAPM: Rf + Œ≤(Rm - Rf))
  Rd = Cost of debt (interest rate)
  T = Tax rate

CAPM Components:
  Rf = Risk-free rate (10-year Treasury)
  Œ≤ (Beta) = Stock's volatility vs. market
  Rm - Rf = Market risk premium (~5-6%)

    Risk Management

    Risk management involves identifying, assessing, and controlling threats to capital and earnings.

    Types of Financial Risk

    • Market Risk - Losses from market movements
    • Credit Risk - Counterparty fails to meet obligations
    • Liquidity Risk - Unable to sell assets quickly at fair price
    • Operational Risk - Failures in processes, systems, or people
    • Currency Risk - Exchange rate fluctuations
    • Interest Rate Risk - Changes in interest rates affect values

    Risk Metrics

    • Value at Risk (VaR) - Maximum expected loss at confidence level
    • Beta - Volatility relative to market
    • Standard Deviation - Total volatility measure
    • Sharpe Ratio - Risk-adjusted return
    • Maximum Drawdown - Largest peak-to-trough decline

    Hedging Strategies

    • Options - Puts protect against downside
    • Futures - Lock in prices
    • Diversification - Reduce unsystematic risk
    • Asset Allocation - Mix of stocks, bonds, alternatives

    Personal Finance Fundamentals

    Personal finance is managing your own financial resources effectively for short and long-term goals.

    Budgeting

    Track income and expenses. Popular methods:

    • 50/30/20 Rule - 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings
    • Zero-Based - Every dollar has a job
    • Envelope System - Cash in category envelopes

    Emergency Fund

    3-6 months of expenses in liquid savings before investing.

    Retirement Planning

    • 401(k) - Employer-sponsored, often with match (free money!)
    • IRA/Roth IRA - Individual retirement accounts with tax advantages
    • 4% Rule - Withdraw 4% of portfolio annually in retirement

    Debt Management

    • High-interest debt first - Credit cards before mortgage
    • Avalanche Method - Pay highest interest first
    • Snowball Method - Pay smallest balance first (psychological wins)
    ‚úÖ

    Priority Order

    1) Emergency fund, 2) Match employer 401(k), 3) Pay high-interest debt, 4) Max retirement accounts, 5) Taxable investing. Automate everything.

    Financial Markets & Economics

    Understanding how markets and the economy work provides context for financial decisions.

    Market Structure

    • Primary Market - New securities issued (IPOs)
    • Secondary Market - Trading existing securities (exchanges)
    • OTC Markets - Direct trading between parties

    Economic Indicators

    • GDP - Total economic output
    • Inflation (CPI) - Price level changes
    • Unemployment Rate - Labor market health
    • Interest Rates - Cost of borrowing, set by central banks
    • Yield Curve - Relationship between bond yields and maturities
