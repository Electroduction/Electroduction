    Introduction to the Legal System

    The legal system is the framework of rules, institutions, and processes that govern society. Understanding its structure is essential for legal work.

    Sources of Law

    • Constitutional Law - Supreme law establishing government structure and rights
    • Statutory Law - Laws enacted by legislative bodies
    • Case Law (Common Law) - Law developed through court decisions
    • Administrative Law - Rules and regulations from government agencies

    Court System Hierarchy

    The U.S. has both federal and state court systems:

    Federal Courts

    • U.S. Supreme Court - Highest court, final arbiter of federal law
    • Courts of Appeals - 13 circuits, hear appeals from district courts
    • District Courts - Trial courts for federal cases
    • Specialized Courts - Bankruptcy, tax, claims

    State Courts

    • State Supreme Court - Highest state court
    • Appellate Courts - Intermediate appeals
    • Trial Courts - General jurisdiction
    • Limited Jurisdiction Courts - Small claims, traffic, family
    üí°

    Jurisdiction

    Jurisdiction determines which court can hear a case. It depends on geography, subject matter, and monetary amount in controversy.

    Legal Research Methods

    Legal research is the process of finding legal authorities to support legal arguments and decisions.

    Primary vs. Secondary Sources

    Primary Sources (Binding Authority)

    • Constitutions - Federal and state
    • Statutes - United States Code (U.S.C.), state codes
    • Case Law - Published court decisions
    • Regulations - Code of Federal Regulations (CFR)

    Secondary Sources (Persuasive)

    • Legal Encyclopedias - Am. Jur., C.J.S.
    • Treatises - In-depth subject analysis
    • Law Reviews - Academic articles
    • Restatements - ALI summaries of common law

    Research Databases

    • Westlaw - Comprehensive legal database, KeyCite citator
    • LexisNexis - Legal database, Shepard's citator
    • Google Scholar - Free case law access
    • CourtListener - Free court opinions
    • Justia - Free legal information

    Citation Checking

    Always verify authorities are still good law using citators:

    • KeyCite (Westlaw) - Red/yellow flags indicate negative treatment
    • Shepard's (Lexis) - Shows subsequent history and citing references
    Citation Format Examples
    Case: Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483 (1954)

    Contract Law Fundamentals

    A contract is a legally enforceable agreement between two or more parties.

    Elements of a Valid Contract

    • Offer - Clear proposal to enter into agreement
    • Acceptance - Unequivocal agreement to offer terms
    • Consideration - Exchange of value (money, services, promises)
    • Capacity - Parties must have legal ability to contract
    • Legality - Purpose must be lawful

    Contract Interpretation

    Rules for interpreting contract language:

    • Plain Meaning Rule - Words given ordinary meaning
    • Whole Contract - Read in context of entire agreement
    • Specific over General - Specific provisions control
    • Contra Proferentem - Ambiguity construed against drafter

    Common Contract Clauses

    • Indemnification - One party protects other from losses
    • Limitation of Liability - Caps on damages
    • Force Majeure - Excuses performance for extraordinary events
    • Governing Law - Which state's law applies
    • Dispute Resolution - Arbitration vs. litigation
    • Non-Compete/Non-Disclosure - Restrictive covenants

    Breach and Remedies

    Breach occurs when a party fails to perform. Remedies include:

    • Compensatory Damages - Put injured party in position if performed
    • Consequential Damages - Foreseeable indirect losses
    • Specific Performance - Court orders performance (rare)
    • Rescission - Cancel contract, restore parties to pre-contract state

    Civil Litigation Process

    Litigation is the process of taking legal action through the court system.

    Stages of Civil Litigation

    1. Pre-Litigation

    • Investigation and evidence gathering
    • Demand letters
    • Settlement negotiations

    2. Pleadings

    • Complaint - Plaintiff's initial filing stating claims
    • Answer - Defendant's response to allegations
    • Counterclaims/Cross-claims - Defendant's claims

    3. Discovery

    Discovery is the formal exchange of information between parties:

    • Interrogatories - Written questions
    • Requests for Production - Documents and things
    • Requests for Admission - Admit/deny facts
    • Depositions - Sworn testimony under oath
    • E-Discovery - Electronic data discovery

    4. Pre-Trial Motions

    • Motion to Dismiss - Case has no legal basis
    • Summary Judgment - No genuine dispute of material fact
    • Motion in Limine - Exclude evidence at trial

    5. Trial

    • Jury selection (if applicable)
    • Opening statements
    • Presentation of evidence
    • Closing arguments
    • Verdict

    6. Post-Trial/Appeals

    • Post-trial motions
    • Appeal to higher court
    • Enforcement of judgment

    Regulatory Compliance

    Regulatory compliance means adhering to laws, regulations, and guidelines relevant to business operations.

    Major Regulatory Areas

    Employment Law

    • Title VII - Prohibits employment discrimination
    • FLSA - Minimum wage, overtime, child labor
    • ADA - Disability accommodation
    • FMLA - Family and medical leave

    Data Privacy

    • GDPR - EU data protection
    • CCPA/CPRA - California privacy rights
    • HIPAA - Health information privacy

    Financial Regulations

    • SOX - Corporate governance, financial reporting
    • Dodd-Frank - Financial system reform
    • AML/BSA - Anti-money laundering

    Environmental

    • EPA regulations - Environmental protection
    • OSHA - Workplace safety

    Compliance Program Elements

    • Written policies and procedures
    • Training and communication
    • Monitoring and auditing
    • Reporting mechanisms (hotlines)
    • Corrective action procedures
    • Documentation and recordkeeping

    Intellectual Property Basics

    Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind protected by law.

    Types of IP Protection

    Patents

    Protect inventions - utility, design, or plant patents. 20-year term for utility patents.

    Trademarks

    Protect brand identifiers - names, logos, slogans. Can last indefinitely with use and renewal.

    Copyrights

    Protect original creative works - writing, music, art, software. Life + 70 years for individuals.

    Trade Secrets

    Protect confidential business information. No registration; requires reasonable secrecy measures.

    Paralegal Skills & Practice

    Paralegals assist attorneys with legal work under attorney supervision. They cannot give legal advice or represent clients in court.

    Core Paralegal Duties

    • Legal research and writing
    • Document drafting and review
    • Case management and organization
    • Client communication (supervised)
    • Discovery assistance
    • Court filing and calendaring

    Document Drafting Tips

    • Use templates - Start with firm templates
    • Define terms - Capitalize and define key terms
    • Be precise - Avoid ambiguity
    • Proofread - Check names, dates, numbers
    • Track changes - Document revisions

    Ethics Rules

    • Confidentiality - Protect client information
    • Conflicts of Interest - Avoid representing adverse parties
    • Competence - Work within abilities
    • Supervision - Work under attorney direction
    • Unauthorized Practice - Cannot practice law
